Step 3 of 9 Who you see

Who do you see?

Why we ask: Patients who feel their therapist truly understands their life stage stay in therapy longer and report stronger alliance early on. Only pick groups where you have real depth — vague-fit matches drop out fast and damage both sides of the relationship.

Source: Norcross & Lambert (2019), Psychotherapy Relationships That Work, 3rd ed. — meta-analytic review.

Step 4 of 9 Specialties

Your specialties

Why we ask: Specialty fit is one of the strongest controllable predictors of a patient staying past session 3. Listing every issue you "could see" instead of what you genuinely treat well backfires — patients leave feeling unseen, and our matcher learns to weight your profile down.

Source: Owen et al. (2016), Counselling & Psychotherapy Research — psychotherapy retention rates and reasons for early termination.

Tier 1 — your top 1–2. The issues you have the most depth in.

Tier 2 — strong secondary (up to 3). You've treated these often and can handle complex cases.

Tier 3 — general (up to 5). Within your scope but not your wheelhouse.

Step 5 of 9 Format & locations

Where & how you work

Why we ask: Schedule and format mismatches drive a large share of first-session no-shows. List only what you can reliably offer — over-listing hurts your reliability score in the matcher, under-listing means you miss great-fit patients you could actually help.

Source: TheraVoca matcher internals + Wampold (2015), The Great Psychotherapy Debate — operational fit accounts for more variance than therapy type.

Step 6 of 9 Insurance & rates

How patients pay

Why we ask: Cost is the most common surprise that ends a would-be therapy relationship at the intro call. Accurate rates + insurance up-front prevents the "I thought you took my plan" disconnect that wastes your time and the patient's. We filter matches by what you actually accept — your honest list = better-fit referrals, fewer dead-end calls.

Source: SAMHSA (2023) National Survey on Drug Use and Health — cost cited as leading reason for not entering or continuing mental health treatment.

Step 7 of 9 Style & bio

How would you describe your style?

Why we ask: Therapeutic alliance — the patient's sense of fit with you — accounts for roughly 30% of treatment outcome variance. That's more than the specific modality you use. Your bio + style tags are the patient's first signal of alliance fit. Write to your real style, not a marketing voice. The patients who pick you because the bio rings true are the ones who stay.

Source: Norcross & Lambert (2019), Psychotherapy Relationships That Work, 3rd ed. — meta-analytic estimate of alliance contribution to outcome variance.

Step 8 of 9 Style fit (deep match)

Deep-match signals

Why we ask: Match on lived experience overlap meaningfully boosts rapport in the first 3 sessions — the window where most early dropout happens. Specific, honest answers here help our matcher surface the patients you're best-positioned to help. Vague or generic answers score neutral — you miss those matches.

Sources: Cabral & Smith (2011), Journal of Counseling Psychology — meta-analysis on matching effects in cross-cultural counseling. Owen et al. (2016) on early-session dropout.
